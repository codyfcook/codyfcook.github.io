Human Capital and the Direction of Science

with Maya Durvasula
[abstract]
Across domains of basic science, researchers study a small fraction of potential topics. We investigate the factors that shape scientist project choice to assess whether this empirical regularity reflects any form of inefficiency. We develop a framework in which scientist project choice is affected by three factors: the cost of acquiring human capital, the cost of acquiring physical capital, and the expected payoffs associated with successful effort. While fixed costs and payoffs are comparable across researchers, the cost of human capital is a function of the scientist’s existing stock of expertise, which is primarily accumulated during their period of initial training and apprenticeship. This generates the prediction that scientists behaving optimally select projects in close proximity to their existing knowledge. As a result, they select research topics in close intellectual proximity to the work of the researchers who trained them, except when projects are expected to be sufficiently high impact. Future work will focus on understanding how this intergenerational transmission of research agendas affects the aggregate distribution of research. We build data to test these predictions in the field of human genomics, where roughly 10 percent of the 20,000 protein-coding human genes are actively studied and where researchers have raised concerns about “missing” investments in potentially important genes. We discuss three policy levers that may be effective in ameliorating the concentration of research effort on a small number of topics: funding for trainees that allows for greater exploration, targeted subsidies intended to reduce the fixed costs of novel projects, and shifts in the rewards system generated by peer review and grant funding.
